## How Spain’s property market works

Spain doesn’t use escrow in the same way as the US or UK. Private contracts control the process until the public deed is signed at the notary. Agents often represent the seller, so hiring an independent lawyer is essential. All deposits you pay before completion are protected only if the contract spells out contingencies.

## Regional highlights for foreign buyers

* **Madrid.** Strong rental demand, especially around Chamartín and Salamanca. Expect 6–10% purchase taxes.
* **Barcelona.** Premium prices and strict licensing for short-term rentals. Ensure the property complies with urban planning rules.
* **Valencia.** Growing expat community, competitive pricing, and favourable lifestyle metrics.
* **Andalusia.** Málaga and Marbella remain popular for sunshine seekers; new tax reductions make purchases more affordable.
* **Balearic Islands.** Limited supply pushes prices up. Pay attention to coastal law restrictions and community rules.

## Due diligence before signing anything

1. **Nota simple.** Extract from the Land Registry detailing ownership, charges, and urban classification.
2. **IBI and community fees.** Request proof that the seller has paid municipal tax (IBI) and community fees.
3. **Urban planning certificates.** Check that the property complies with local planning regulations, especially for rural homes.
4. **Utility status.** Confirm there are no outstanding bills for electricity, water, or gas.
5. **Energy certificate.** Required for marketing; ratings influence renovation planning.

## Reservation and deposit contracts

* **Reserva.** A short agreement (often €3,000–€6,000) that takes the property off the market for a few weeks.
* **Contrato de arras.** Binding deposit contract, usually 10% of the purchase price. In Spain, *arras penitenciales* allow either party to exit: the buyer loses the deposit if they withdraw, the seller repays double if they cancel.

Ensure the contract includes:

* Condition that the property is free of liens at completion.
* Timeline for completing the deed.
* Return of the deposit if the mortgage is refused despite a formal application.
* Obligations for the seller to deliver occupancy certificates or first occupancy licence for new builds.

## Mortgages and banking for expats

Non-resident buyers typically qualify for 60–70% loan-to-value with amortisations up to 25 years. Steps:

1. **Secure your NIE.** Banks cannot issue the mortgage without it.
2. **Open a non-resident account.** Provide passport, proof of address, employment contract, and non-resident certificate.
3. **Submit financial documents.** Recent tax returns, payslips, or company accounts. Translate and legalise if required.
4. **Bank valuation (*tasación*).** Costs €300–€600 and determines the maximum loan.
5. **Mortgage offer (FEIN/FIAE).** Spanish regulations grant you ten days to review terms before signing at the notary.

Interest rates in early 2025 range between 3.5–4.5% fixed, with mixed-rate products gaining popularity. Factor in arrangement fees (0.5–1%) and life or home insurance requirements.

## Closing at the notary

The public deed (*escritura pública*) is signed before a notary. Expect:

* **Document review.** Notary confirms identities, property status, and payment method.
* **Payment.** Provide banker’s drafts or proof of bank transfer. Spanish rules require anti-money laundering checks.
* **Registration.** After signing, the deed is submitted to the Land Registry. Registration takes 1–3 months.

If using a mortgage, the bank’s representative attends and signs simultaneously. We pre-brief the notary and prepare bilingual summaries so you understand every clause.

## Taxes and ongoing costs

| Scenario | Tax | Rate |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Resale property | Transfer Tax (ITP) | 6–10% depending on region |
| New build | VAT (IVA) | 10% on residential property |
| New build | Stamp Duty (AJD) | 1–1.5% depending on region |
| Mortgage | AJD | 0.5–1.5% paid by the bank under current rules |
| Annual ownership | IBI municipal tax | Varies by municipality |
| Rental income | Non-resident tax (Modelo 210) | 19% for EU/EEA, 24% otherwise |

Budget for community fees (for shared facilities), property insurance, and potential wealth tax depending on region and net assets.

## Using power of attorney

If you cannot travel for each appointment, grant a Spanish notarial power of attorney (PoA) to a trusted representative. The PoA can cover:

* Signing the *arras* contract
* Opening bank accounts and applying for mortgages
* Completing the notary deed
* Registering utilities and taxes

Foreign PoAs must be notarised, apostilled, and translated. Juro Spain drafts bilingual PoAs that meet Spanish standards and arranges local notarisation or embassy appointments abroad.

## Post-completion checklist

1. **Land Registry confirmation.** Receive the updated title reflecting your ownership.
2. **Tax filings.** Submit ITP/IVA/AJD within 30 working days and register for non-resident tax if applicable.
3. **Utility transfers.** Change electricity, water, and gas contracts to your name.
4. **Community onboarding.** Notify the building administrator, share contact details, and set up direct debits.
5. **Insurance and security.** Update home insurance, install alarms if necessary, and schedule maintenance checks.
6. **Residency planning.** If pursuing the Golden Visa or another residency permit, integrate property documents into your immigration file.

## Avoiding common pitfalls

* **Skipping surveys.** Older properties may hide structural issues. Commission a technical survey to budget for repairs.
* **Underestimating taxes.** Include ITP or IVA, AJD, legal fees, and furniture budgets in your financial plan.
* **Incomplete bank paperwork.** Missing translations delay mortgage drawdowns.
* **Overlooking community rules.** Some buildings restrict holiday rentals or renovations; review statutes before buying.
* **Ignoring inheritance planning.** Consider Spanish inheritance tax implications and prepare wills in Spain and your home country.

## FAQs

### Can foreigners buy property in Spain without residency?

Yes. Non-residents can buy freely. You only need an NIE and must comply with anti-money laundering checks.

### How long does the purchase take?

Typically 6–10 weeks from reservation to completion, depending on mortgage approvals and document turnaround.

### Do I need a Spanish bank account?

Yes, for most purchases. Sellers and notaries prefer local banker’s drafts, and direct debits for utilities require a Spanish IBAN.

### What if the seller has outstanding debts?

Debts registered against the property (e.g., mortgages) transfer with the property. Ensure the notary deed states the seller will cancel charges on completion and hold funds in escrow if necessary.

### Can I buy through a company?

Yes, but expect extra compliance. Provide company statutes, shareholder information, and ultimate beneficial owner declarations.
