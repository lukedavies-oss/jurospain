Spain’s Digital Nomad Visa (Visado para Teletrabajadores de Carácter Internacional) is now a mature route for remote professionals and founders. With clear income thresholds and streamlined processing, it offers a bridge to Spanish residency without abandoning your foreign employer. This 2025 guide breaks down the requirements, documents, taxes, and real-world timelines we see in client files.

## Who the Digital Nomad Visa is for

Designed for remote employees and entrepreneurs whose clients are predominantly outside Spain, the permit grants an initial one-year visa (when applied for abroad) or a three-year residence card (when applied from Spain). It fits:

* Salaried professionals employed by companies outside Spain.
* Freelancers with foreign clients (up to 20% of income may come from Spanish clients).
* Founders of startups incorporated abroad who can demonstrate an active business and remote operability.

## Eligibility checklist for 2025

* University degree, higher vocational qualification, or three years of relevant experience.
* Employment or professional relationship with the foreign company for at least three months before applying.
* Company operating for at least one year and authorising remote work from Spain.
* Clean criminal record for the past five years.
* Private health insurance that covers Spain with zero co-payments.
* Proof of sufficient funds: 200% of the Spanish minimum wage (€2,520/month) for the main applicant plus 75% (€945/month) per additional adult and 25% (€315/month) per child.

## Income thresholds and acceptable evidence

Show regular income via:

* Employment contract outlining salary, remote-work authorisation, and duties.
* Payslips or invoices from the last three to six months.
* Bank statements proving consistent deposits.
* If self-employed: service contracts with clients, invoices, and proof of continued engagement.

For founders, include corporate documents showing you own less than 50% of the Spanish entity (to maintain non-resident status) and that the foreign company has substance (team, revenue, or product).

## Documents you need (and how to prep them)

1. **Passport** valid for at least one year.
2. **Proof of qualifications** (degree certificates) apostilled and translated.
3. **Employment documentation**—contracts, letters from HR confirming remote status, and proof of tenure.
4. **Financial evidence**—bank statements, invoices, or payslips.
5. **Criminal record certificate** from countries lived in during the past five years (apostilled and translated).
6. **Health insurance policy** with full coverage in Spain.
7. **Application forms**: national visa form or Form EX-01 (if applying inside Spain) plus Form 790 Código 052 for residence authorisation fees.
8. **Declaration of absence of criminal records** signed before a notary (some consulates require).
9. **Letter of motivation** detailing your role, how remote work will be performed, and the impact on Spain.

Prepare two sets of documents: originals plus copies. Consulates often require appointments booked via their online portals, so secure a slot as early as possible.

## Application routes: consulate vs Spain

**Consulate route:**

* Submit in your home country or country of legal residence.
* Receive a one-year visa, then apply for the TIE within 30 days of arriving in Spain.

**In-country route:**

* Enter Spain on a tourist visa and file telematically with UGE-CE.
* Receive a three-year residence authorisation, then book fingerprinting for the TIE.

We evaluate both options based on your travel plans, current residency status, and family needs. The in-country route requires careful timing to submit before your 90-day stay ends.

## Health insurance and background checks

Health insurance must be issued by a Spanish provider (e.g., Adeslas, Sanitas) with no co-payments or grace periods. Policies should cover the entire family. Travel insurance is insufficient.

Criminal record certificates must be issued within 90 days of submission. Arrange apostilles early and use sworn translators in Spain. If you lived in multiple countries, gather certificates from each—digital versions may be acceptable if they include verification codes.

## Tax implications and Beckham regime

Digital nomad visa holders can opt into Spain’s Special Expatriate Tax Regime (known as the Beckham Law) if they become tax residents. Benefits include a flat 24% rate on Spanish employment income up to €600,000 and exemption on most foreign-source income for six years. Requirements:

* Move to Spain for work reasons.
* Not have been a Spanish tax resident in the previous five years.
* Obtain the regime within six months of registering with Social Security (if applicable).

We coordinate with tax advisors to decide whether the regime suits your profile. Even without Beckham, you may remain a non-resident for tax if you spend fewer than 183 days per year in Spain and keep your centre of economic interests abroad. However, enrolling children in Spanish schools or purchasing property may tilt the balance, so assess holistically.

## Family members and schooling

You can include:

* Spouse or registered partner.
* Children under 18 and older dependent children.
* Dependent parents in exceptional cases.

Each dependant must provide health insurance, birth/marriage certificates, and financial evidence (extra funds per dependant). International schools in Madrid, Barcelona, and Valencia have waiting lists—apply as soon as your move is confirmed. For public schools, register with the local town hall once you have proof of address.

## Timeline from idea to residency card

1. **Weeks 1–2:** Eligibility review, document collection, notarised authorisations.
2. **Weeks 3–4:** Apostilles, sworn translations, health insurance activation.
3. **Week 5:** Submit visa application or telematic file.
4. **Weeks 6–10:** Resolution (UGE often responds in 20 working days; consulates vary by country).
5. **Week 11:** Travel to Spain if using the visa route; register at the town hall (empadronamiento).
6. **Week 12:** Fingerprinting appointment for the TIE; card ready in 30–45 days.

We provide a detailed timeline with reminders for each milestone, ensuring you never miss a deadline.

## Common mistakes and how to avoid them

* **Insufficient income proof.** Include multiple evidence types—contracts, pay slips, bank statements—to avoid doubts about stability.
* **Using travel insurance.** Consulates reject policies that are not full medical coverage with zero co-payments.
* **Late background checks.** Certificates older than 90 days are invalid. Schedule them near submission and courier originals promptly.
* **Ignoring tax planning.** Decide early if you will become tax resident and whether to apply for the Beckham regime.
* **Missing the TIE window.** Once in Spain, book fingerprinting immediately—appointments fill fast in major cities.

## FAQs

### Can I work for Spanish clients?

Yes, but income from Spanish companies or clients must not exceed 20% of your total revenue to keep the digital nomad status.

### Do I need to register as autónomo?

Salaried employees usually stay on foreign payrolls. Freelancers may register with Spanish Social Security if invoicing Spanish clients; our team coordinates the best approach.

### How long can I stay outside Spain?

You can travel freely but should spend at least 183 days per year in Spain if you want to maintain tax residency or progress toward permanent residency.

### Can the visa lead to permanent residency?

Yes. After five years of continuous legal stay (renewing the nomad permit), you can apply for long-term EU residency, assuming you meet stay requirements.

### What happens if I change employers?

Notify the immigration authorities and submit documentation from the new employer demonstrating remote work and financial stability. Changes within the first three months may trigger additional scrutiny.
