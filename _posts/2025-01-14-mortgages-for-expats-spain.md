Securing a Spanish mortgage as an expat is achievable with the right preparation. Banks analyse your global income, debt-to-income ratio, and property details, so presenting a polished file matters. This guide outlines the 2025 lending landscape, key documents, and how to stay on schedule.

## Which banks lend to expats

* **Banco Sabadell:** Long-standing expat lender with English-speaking teams and flexible property coverage.
* **CaixaBank:** Offers fixed and variable rates, strong for buyers in Catalonia and Valencia.
* **Bankinter:** Competitive mixed-rate mortgages and fast underwriting for high-income professionals.
* **BBVA:** Digital document submission and attractive rates for stable salaried applicants.
* **Cajasur, Unicaja, Ibercaja:** Regional banks open to expats purchasing within their territories.
* **International brokers:** Independent brokers (e.g., Mortgage Direct, Lionsgate) can access boutique bank offers and negotiate better rates.

## Loan-to-value ratios and affordability

* **Non-residents:** Up to 70% loan-to-value (LTV) for primary or holiday homes; sometimes 60% for rural or high-risk properties.
* **Residents:** Up to 80–90% LTV depending on income stability and credit profile.

Banks evaluate affordability by ensuring your total worldwide debt payments stay below 35–40% of your net monthly income. Provide a full picture of loans, credit cards, and existing mortgages.

## Interest rate trends in 2025

* Fixed rates: 3.4–4.2% depending on term and profile.
* Variable rates: Euribor (currently ~2.7%) + 1.0–1.5% margin.
* Mixed rates: Fixed for the first 3–10 years, then switch to variable.

Spanish banks often offer better rates when you bundle products (home insurance, life insurance, credit cards). Compare the overall cost before accepting add-ons.

## Documents you’ll need

* Passport and NIE.
* Proof of income:
  * Employees: last three payslips, annual tax return (P60, 1040, etc.), employment contract.
  * Self-employed: last two tax returns, profit and loss statements, accountant letter.
* Bank statements covering six months.
* Existing debt statements.
* Reservation or deposit contract (*arras*) for the property.
* Property details: nota simple, cadastral reference, purchase price.
* Non-resident certificate (if applicable).

Provide certified translations if documents aren’t in Spanish or English. Banks appreciate well-organised PDFs labelled by category.

## Timeline from application to completion

1. **Week 1:** Collect documents, run affordability check with your advisor.
2. **Week 2:** Submit application; receive pre-approval within 5–10 working days.
3. **Week 3–4:** Bank orders valuation; you pay the valuation fee.
4. **Week 5:** Mortgage offer issued (FEIN/FIAE). Spanish law grants you 10 calendar days to review.
5. **Week 6:** Sign mortgage deed at the notary alongside the purchase deed.

Allow extra time for public holidays and August slowdowns. We keep lenders updated with property timelines to avoid last-minute delays.

## Understanding valuations (*tasación*)

* Conducted by an independent, bank-approved valuation company.
* Determines the maximum LTV; if valuation is lower than purchase price, you must cover the difference.
* Valid for six months (extendable by two months with confirmation).
* Cost: €300–€600 depending on property type.

Attend the valuation if possible to highlight renovations or upgrades that justify the price.

## Insurance and ancillary products

Spanish banks often require:

* **Home insurance:** Fire and contents cover naming the bank as beneficiary.
* **Life insurance:** Optional but may reduce rates. Assess if external policies offer better value.
* **Payment protection:** Less common in 2025 but may be offered for self-employed borrowers.

Review policy terms carefully—some banks tie promotional rates to maintaining insurance for the loan’s duration.

## Common issues and how to solve them

* **Currency mismatch:** If income is not in euros, banks may apply conservative exchange rates. Provide long-term income history to demonstrate stability.
* **Complex income structures:** Company directors or freelancers should submit accountant letters and tax breakdowns to clarify dividends vs salary.
* **Low valuation:** Negotiate the purchase price, add more equity, or approach another bank willing to consider the contract price.
* **Tight deadlines:** Align *arras* completion dates with mortgage approval buffers (minimum 45 days).
* **Documentation gaps:** Use a cloud folder with labelled files. Respond to bank requests within 24 hours to keep underwriting moving.

## FAQs

### Can I get a mortgage without residency?

Yes. Non-residents can obtain up to 70% LTV mortgages provided they meet income and credit requirements.

### Do Spanish banks check international credit scores?

They focus on your provided documentation and may run checks in Spain. Some request credit reports from your home country (Experian, Equifax, etc.).

### How early should I apply?

Submit documents as soon as you sign the reservation contract. Pre-approvals can be valid for 3–6 months.

### Can I pay off the mortgage early?

Yes, but expect early repayment fees: typically 0.15% in the first five years and 0.0–0.15% thereafter for variable loans; fixed loans may charge up to 2%.

### What if I plan to rent the property?

Inform the bank. Some lenders restrict holiday rentals or adjust rates. Ensure your mortgage contract permits rental activity.
