Registering on the municipal padrón (empadronamiento) is one of the first steps after moving to Spain. It proves where you live, grants access to public services, and is often required for visa renewals, school enrolment, and healthcare. This guide walks you through the process in 2025, from booking appointments to collecting the certificate.

## What is empadronamiento?

Empadronamiento is Spain’s municipal census. It records residents living in each town or city. The certificate you receive, the *volante* or *certificado de empadronamiento*, is the official proof of address that many institutions request.

## Who must register and when

* Anyone living in Spain for more than 183 days per year must register in their municipality.
* EU citizens should register within three months of arrival (often alongside EU residency certificates).
* Non-EU residents must usually provide proof of empadronamiento when applying for the TIE or renewing visas such as the non-lucrative or digital nomad permits.

Register even if you plan a short stay. Failing to register can complicate healthcare access, school enrolment, and municipal services.

## Documents you’ll need

* Passport or national ID for each applicant.
* NIE or residency card (if available).
* Proof of address:
  * Rental contract registered with the regional housing authority.
  * Property deed (*escritura*) or recent IBI receipt.
  * If you’re staying with friends, a signed authorisation from the homeowner plus their ID and proof of ownership.
* Completed padrón form (download from your town hall website).
* For families: birth certificates of children and, if parents are separated, custody documents.

Bring originals and copies. Some municipalities require contracts to be in Spanish; provide translations if necessary.

## Booking the appointment

Most large cities use online booking portals. Search for “cita previa padrón” plus your city name (Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia, Málaga). Smaller towns often allow walk-ins during office hours.

When booking, select the correct procedure (alta en padrón, modificación, or baja). Choose a date and time, then download the confirmation PDF. Print or save it to your phone.

## On the day: what to expect

1. Arrive 10–15 minutes early with all applicants present.
2. Take a ticket if the office operates a queue system.
3. Present documents to the clerk; they will verify the address and update the municipal database.
4. Sign the registration form digitally or on paper.

Some offices issue the certificate immediately; others email it within a few hours. Verify the spelling of names and addresses before leaving.

## Getting your padrón certificate

You can request copies online or in person. Certificates are valid for:

* 3 months for immigration procedures.
* 6 months for general admin tasks.

Use the certificate for:

* TIE applications and renewals.
* School enrolment (provide proof of residence in the catchment area).
* Public healthcare registration (Tarjeta Sanitaria).
* Social services and voting in local elections (for EU citizens).

## How it connects to healthcare and schooling

* **Healthcare:** Present the padrón certificate at your local health centre along with your Social Security number (if applicable) to obtain a health card.
* **Schooling:** Local schools ask for the padrón to confirm your address for enrolment priority. International schools may also request it for their records.
* **Benefits:** Municipal grants or cultural programmes may require proof of empadronamiento.

## Renewals and updates

* EU citizens generally renew every five years or when changing address.
* Non-EU residents should update the padrón whenever they move. Some municipalities request revalidation every two years to confirm you still live there.
* If you leave Spain for more than six months, consider deregistering to avoid municipal fines.

## Troubleshooting common issues

* **Landlord won’t authorise registration.** Spanish law obliges property owners to cooperate. Offer to include a clause in the lease or provide them with the municipal forms showing it carries no tax penalty.
* **Short-term rentals.** Many town halls accept medium-term rental contracts (3–11 months). Provide utility bills or an empadronamiento authorisation from the owner.
* **Name mismatches.** Ensure your passport and lease use the same spelling. Bring marriage certificates if your surname differs.
* **Delayed certificates.** Follow up via email or return to the office. Some municipalities allow online downloads using the appointment code.

## FAQs

### Can I register without a long-term lease?

Yes. Use a medium-term rental contract, hotel invoice, or owner authorisation. Provide utility bills where possible to reinforce your proof.

### Do children need to attend?

Many town halls allow parents to register children with their documentation, but some request the child’s presence. Check the local policy.

### Is empadronamiento linked to taxes?

No. It does not automatically make you a tax resident, but it is often used as supporting evidence alongside days of presence.

### How soon can I get the certificate?

Often on the same day. Busy cities may email it within 24 hours.

### What if I move within the same city?

Update the padrón with your new address. Bring the new lease or deed and request a modification appointment.
